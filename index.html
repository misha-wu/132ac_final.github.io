India: Sociopolitical context/country history

India possesses a distinctive global position characterized by its status as a secular nation intertwined deeply with religion, a nation marked by intricate colonial legacies, and a nation grappling with intense identity politics amid significant state socio-political influence. Muslims in India hold a distinctive position as one of the nation's largest and historically entrenched minority groups, making up around 15% of the countries population.

India lies in the heartland of South Asia, bordering countries such as Pakistan, Bangladesh, China, Myanmar in modern-day; thanks to its coastal trade ports and proximity to popular inland routes, India has been a long-existing hub of cultural diversity.

In pre-colonial times, India was ruled by different dynasties of rulers, such as the Maurya Empire, Gupta Empire, and the Mughal Empire. Thus, unlike other nation-states of the era, India experienced persistent mixing of cultures and nationalities, leading to a stronger sense of identity within each group. The Mughal Empire was, specifically, a long era of Muslim rule, only declining by the late 17th century due to a mix of external and internal factors. Consequently, Islam has been a persistent force in India for many centuries, leaving a rich legacy of history, culture, and architecture unique to the Indian subcontinent; however, it also left behind great tension between Hindus and Muslims, which would be taken advantage of and exacerbated in later centuries [Maizland].

India was then subsequently colonized under British rule, forming British Raj; this lasted from 1858 until 1947, in which India and Pakistan gained independence.

Post-British colonization, India established a secular government that followed traditional European infrastructure; however, this move did not alleviate complex and long-standing religious and political relationships between its many minority groups ‚Äî including Hindus vs Muslims.

Many events further exacerbated discontent along racial lines.

The Partition of India (1947) [Minority Rights Group International.]

With growing unrest at the turn of the century, India was forcibly partitioned into the states of India and Pakistan in a bid for religious separation. However, this move was fraught with violence, uprooting and displacing millions of Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs, proving to be a deadly and traumatic memory in the lives of many modern-day Indians. In turn, this partition became a cause of resentment and anger for both sides, even establishing a precedent that certain groups do not belong in this country at all.

The Emergency (1975-1977)

The Emergency refers to a period of civil unrest under Indian Prime Minister Indira Gandhi; Gandhi utilized authoritarian methods and suspended civil liberties to suppress dissenting voices within the government. Consequently, her campaign targeted and suppressed minorities, leading to cruel and inhumane acts such as media censorship, forced sterilization of marginalized communities, and persecution of political opponents [bbc].

Communal Riots

India also has a pattern of communal riots in response to extreme and divisive policies; these riots include the 2002 Gujarat Riots, 1989 Bhagalpur Riots, and the 2020 Delhi riots, among many others. These riots arise out of polarization and anger, and often target religious minorities such as Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, and Christians; these violent outbreaks subsequently result result in loss of life, damage to property, and worsen distrust among different communities, creating a vicious cycle.

5. Legal restrictions on Muslim citizens and immigrants/refugees from Muslim-majority states.

Despite being India‚Äôs largest minority, Muslims are fundamentally limited in rights and representation alike.

The 2019 Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) provided a clear path of nationalization for refugees from six religious minorities ‚Äî Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian ‚Äî but neglected Muslims, an arguably larger portion of neighboring countries. In turn, Muslim refugees from nearby states find it difficult to legally emigrate to India.

Simultaneously, this bill also required current citizens to prove their legality; this proved to be difficult for many minorities, despite living on the land for generations. Muslim families were at risk of being ‚Äústripped of their citizenship rights‚Äù in the name of a unifying citizenship; ultimately, this law drew fire from multiple international organizations such as the International Religious Freedom (USCIRF), the United Nations (UN), and Human Rights Watch for being a cover for government-sanctioned discrimination and disenfranchisement. This also indirectly led to riots over legitimate citizenship, once again propelling a cycle of violence between religious groups [Human Rights Watch].

The reservation system in India, initially introduced by the British government as a form of affirmative action to represent historically disadvantaged groups, extends across various facets of Indian society, including education, employment, and governmental representation. However, despite constituting the largest minority in India, Muslims find themselves excluded from significant representation. Instead, they are categorized under the Other Backward Class (OBC) designation, along with other minorities and lower-caste Hindus ‚Äî despite being the largest minority. OBCs are estimated to make up over 60% of the population, yet fight for 27% of the seats in a reservation system; within OBCs, Muslims have to compete. Ultimately, this leads to a lack of representation and avenues for higher education within government ‚Äî in fact, Muslims hold less than 5% of positions in governing bodies ("Muslims face dwindling representation in Modi's India").

6. Economic restrictions.

While there are no laws that explicitly target Muslim minorities, many states of India establish laws that indirectly and disproportionately affect its Muslim citizens.

One such example include cow protection laws. Hinduism treats cows as sacred and holy; this, many states pass laws that are structured to protect cows. However, many Muslims rely on cattle farming, dairy farming, or meat industry-related roles for economic support. Thus, when laws meant to protect cattle are passed ‚Äî such as banning the cattle trade, or limiting the sale of cattle ‚Äî one group is disproportionately affected compared to the other.

One such example is the Maharashtra Animal Preservation Act of 2015, which prohibited cow slaughtering (Maharashtra Act No. IX of 1977).

Initially presented as legislation aimed at providing economic assistance to industries dependent on cow byproducts, this law carried severe penalties for non-compliance, with punishments ranging from 10 years to life imprisonment, varying based on the statutes of individual states. Furthermore, laws also often put the burden of proof on the accused, resulting in disproportionate prosecution of Muslim cattle ranchers (Patel 260).This gave rise to the idea of ‚Äúcow lynching‚Äù, where mobs violently target minorities in the name of cow protection. Once again, the government passively supports this injustice ‚Äì ‚Äúpolice often stalled prosecutions of the attackers, while several BJP politicians publicly justified the attacks‚Äù (Human Rights Watch).
