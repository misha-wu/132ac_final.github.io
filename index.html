India: A Sociopolitical context and history

India possesses a distinctive global position characterized by its status as a secular nation intertwined deeply with religion, a nation marked by intricate colonial legacies, and a nation grappling with intense identity politics amid significant state socio-political influence. Muslims in India hold a distinctive position as one of the nation's largest and historically entrenched minority groups, making up around 15% of the countries population.

India lies in the heartland of South Asia, bordering countries such as Pakistan, Bangladesh, China, Myanmar in modern-day; thanks to its coastal trade ports and proximity to popular inland routes, India has been a long-existing hub of cultural diversity.

In pre-colonial times, India was ruled by different dynasties of rulers, such as the Maurya Empire, Gupta Empire, and the Mughal Empire. Thus, unlike other nation-states of the era, India experienced persistent mixing of cultures and nationalities, leading to a stronger sense of identity within each group. The Mughal Empire was, specifically, a long era of Muslim rule, only declining by the late 17th century due to a mix of external and internal factors. Consequently, Islam has been a persistent force in India for many centuries, leaving a rich legacy of history, culture, and architecture unique to the Indian subcontinent; however, it also left behind great tension between Hindus and Muslims, which would be taken advantage of and exacerbated in later centuries (Maizland).

India was then subsequently colonized under British rule, forming British Raj; this lasted from 1858 until 1947, in which India and Pakistan gained independence.

Post-British colonization, India established a secular government that followed traditional European infrastructure; however, this move did not alleviate complex and long-standing religious and political relationships between its many minority groups — including Hindus vs Muslims.

Many events further exacerbated discontent along racial lines.

The Partition of India (1947) (Oxford History Dept.)

With growing unrest at the turn of the century, India was forcibly partitioned into the states of India and Pakistan in a bid for religious separation. However, this move was fraught with violence, uprooting and displacing millions of Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs, proving to be a deadly and traumatic memory in the lives of many modern-day Indians. In turn, this partition became a cause of resentment and anger for both sides, even establishing a precedent that certain groups do not belong in this country at all.

The Emergency (1975-1977)

The Emergency refers to a period of civil unrest under Indian Prime Minister Indira Gandhi; Gandhi utilized authoritarian methods and suspended civil liberties to suppress dissenting voices within the government. Consequently, her campaign targeted and suppressed minorities, leading to cruel and inhumane acts such as media censorship, forced sterilization of marginalized communities, and persecution of political opponents (BBC India).

Communal Riots

India also has a pattern of communal riots in response to extreme and divisive policies; these riots include the 2002 Gujarat Riots, 1989 Bhagalpur Riots, and the 2020 Delhi riots, among many others. These riots arise out of polarization and anger, and often target religious minorities such as Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, and Christians; these violent outbreaks subsequently result result in loss of life, damage to property, and worsen distrust among different communities, creating a vicious cycle.

2. Country profile

India, officially known as the second most populated country on Earth, has more than 1.4 billion people. It is a very diversified country, not only in the number of populations with respect to ethnicities and languages, but it is also diversified in terms of religions.

Population:

According to the 2011 census, the major ethnic groups in Nepal are of Indo-Aryan descent, 72%, and Dravidian, 25% (Word Population Review). There is also a presence of Mongoloid and other groups amounting to 3% of the whole populace. Linguistically, it is the most spoken language of Hindi, yet the constitution of India acknowledges 22 regional languages. Apart from Hindi, these include Bengali, Telugu, Marathi, Tamil, Urdu, Gujarati, Malayalam, Kannada, Oriya, Punjabi, and others. English is also highly preferred, mostly used in business and at the time for higher education.

It is populated by about 65% below the age of 35 years. According to the general census, the literacy rate stands at 74% in the year 2011. It is fast-moving on the economic front, and the surging rise of a new middle class, particularly in the urban areas, has been recorded. However, India still remains a developing country. But there are still problems with poverty and inequality facing society, especially in the countryside.

Demographics of Government:

India follows a multi-party system, a federal parliamentary democratic republic. The head of the country is the President at the state level and the Prime Minister at the government level. The Parliament of India consists of two houses: Rajya Sabha (Council of States) and Lok Sabha (House of the People).

Representation of different religious, linguistic, and caste/community groups has been a very crucial factor in Indian politics since independence in 1947. The current ruling party, Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), and the Prime Minister, Narendra Modi, have Hindu nationalist leanings. The only thing that gives hope, nevertheless, is that the parliament and the state legislatures across the country do have representations cutting a wide spectrum of parties and backgrounds.

Religious Diversity:

India is the birthplace of four major world religions: Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism. As per the 2011 Census, religious composition in India:

  • Hindu 79.8%
  • Muslim 14.2%
  • Christian 2.3%
  • Sikh 1.7%
  • Buddhist 0.7%
  • Jain 0.4%
  • Other religions & persuasions 0.9%

(source: Pew Research Center)

Though termed as a secular country by the constitution, religion indeed does play a paramount role in the lives of most of its citizens. This is because it is deeply entwined with politics in most parts of the country. Tensions of Hinduism-majority and Muslim-minority have been part of Indian society for hundreds of years. Lately, they have been cropping up and growing. To put it differently, this is to be considered India, which is home to so many kinds of people speaking innumerable languages and following so many religions. This kind of unity in diversity gets some reflection in the pluralistic democracy of the country; at the same time, it is also giving rise to continuous challenges as India progresses as an independent country because of the existence of such social cleavages and inequalities.

3. Discussion of political figures

Introduction

The last decade has witnessed a severe growth of Islamophobic rhetoric in Indian political discourse, especially during electoral campaigns. Nowhere has this tendency been sharper than in the narratives of some political figures and parties looking to consolidate a certain type of voter.

The paper mainly discusses Prime Minister Narendra Modi and his party, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), along with the roles played by them in making use of Islamophobia as their tool for campaigns.

Narendra Modi Biography:

Narendra Modi, born on September 17, 1950, in Vadnagar, Gujarat, is an Indian politician and the Prime Minister of the country; he was elected to his first term on May 26, 2014 (Brittanica). Before his premiership, Modi had been the Chief Minister of the state of Gujarat from 2001 to 2014. He was a member of Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), a Hindu volunteer organization, and during his time of premiership, he was related to the BJP.

Islamophobia: His policies during his tenure as Prime Minister and his statements have always been such that they cater to Hindu nationalist sentiments, many argue, at the cost of further marginalizing the Muslim communities.

  • His party and he are blamed in particular for using the hostile and anti-Muslim rhetoric of polarization during electoral campaigns. Examples are the National Register of Citizens (NRC) and the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA), as many sections of the society feel that the two are aimed at isolating and discriminating against the Muslims under the pretext of identifying illegal immigrants.

Party Overview:

Established in 1980, Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) is a right-wing Hindu nationalist political party that is one of the two major national parties of India. The party, from its history, has associations with the RSS and is a major force of India in its political setup, especially after it won a major majority in the 2014 national elections.

  • Utilization of Islamophobia: The utilization of Islamophobia by the BJP in its electoral campaigns has been evinced through the very policies and narratives that it often propounds against the Muslim communities. For example, in so many State and National Elections, the party has used narratives that brand Muslims as the 'other,' accusing them of indulging in anti-national activities and their loyalties toward India. They always use such strategies to consolidate their Hindu vote bank and, therefore, they generally have underlying religious identity politics. The election speeches and the party manifestos have often been perceived with a danger from alleged Muslim favoritism that could weaken Hindu culture and society, they say.

Conclusion

The use of Islamophobia in electioneering by the likes of Narendra Modi and his BJP party is just yet another facet of a larger trend unfolding towards religious identity politics within India. This approach has done fairly well for them in trying to consolidate a very substantial chunk of the Hindu vote by fomenting communal divisions. However, at the same time, it raises serious questions about social harmony and the secular fabric of the country. Appreciation of these dynamics becomes inevitable for the analysis of the impact of political strategies on communal relations and democratic setups of the country.

4. Media, Films, and Images.

Like many other countries, Muslims tend to be portrayed in a negative light. One such example is Bollywood film Roja (1992), in which the main characters husband is abducted by Muslim rebels from Kashmir. Muslim men are portrayed as predatory, violent, and cruel; Muslim women are portrayed as sex objects, lacking agency in their own lives. This is a similar phenomenon to what Jack Shaheen observed in the film Reel Bad Arabs, in which he analyzes the stereotypes and negative image of Muslims in American Hollywood (Shaheen). These portrayals are insensitive, inaccurate, and attribute Muslim minorities to a monolith of traits.

However, with the rise of Hindu nationalist ideologies is the government sanctioning of Muslim vilification. One prime example is the film The Kashmir Files (2022), a film describing the forced exodus of Kashmiri Hindus from the Muslim-majority state of Kashmir. In it, Muslims are portrayed as separatist, oppressive, and needlessly cruel, driving out Hindus with a genocide. However, much of the film is actually dramatized and revisionist. Despite the clear sensationalism, the film’s narrative — of Hindus being oppressed by Muslims, of Muslims being a threat national identity — is heavily supported by the BJP, the ruling party of India. In fact, in some states, the BJP even promotes The Kashmir Files by making it tax-free (Hindu Stan Times).

Films like The Kashmir Files have real consequences, to the point of fanning the flames of communal anger. In 2022 Ram Navami riots, Khargone saw violence with rhetoric quoting the movie — "Wake up Hindus, lest other states in India become Kashmir” (”How ‘Kashmir Files’ added to communal fires in Khargone that ended with bulldozer injustice”). Politicians also echo rhetoric from the movie — with Narendra Modhi praising the film for "[revealing] the truth” (”How the BJP is promoting ‘The Kashmir Files’: Modi’s endorsement, tax breaks, leave from work”). Ultimately, Muslims in media becomes a vehicle of both latent and manifest Islamophobia, being a source of stereotyping and political propaganda alike.

5. Legal restrictions on Muslim citizens and immigrants/refugees from Muslim-majority states.

Despite being India’s largest minority, Muslims are fundamentally limited in rights and representation alike.

The 2019 Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) provided a clear path of nationalization for refugees from six religious minorities — Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian — but neglected Muslims, an arguably larger portion of neighboring countries. In turn, Muslim refugees from nearby states find it difficult to legally emigrate to India (BBC News).

Simultaneously, this bill also required current citizens to prove their legality; this proved to be difficult for many minorities, despite living on the land for generations. Muslim families were at risk of being “stripped of their citizenship rights” in the name of a unifying citizenship; ultimately, this law drew fire from multiple international organizations such as the International Religious Freedom (USCIRF), the United Nations (UN), and Human Rights Watch for being a cover for government-sanctioned discrimination and disenfranchisement. This also indirectly led to riots over legitimate citizenship, once again propelling a cycle of violence between religious groups (Human Rights Watch).

The reservation system in India, initially introduced by the British government as a form of affirmative action to represent historically disadvantaged groups, extends across various facets of Indian society, including education, employment, and governmental representation. However, despite constituting the largest minority in India, Muslims find themselves excluded from significant representation. Instead, they are categorized under the Other Backward Class (OBC) designation, along with other minorities and lower-caste Hindus — despite being the largest minority (Pew Research Center). OBCs are estimated to make up over 60% of the population, yet fight for 27% of the seats in a reservation system; within OBCs, Muslims have to compete. Ultimately, this leads to a lack of representation and avenues for higher education within government — in fact, Muslims hold less than 5% of positions in governing bodies ("Muslims face dwindling representation in Modi's India").

6. Economic restrictions.

While there are no laws that explicitly target Muslim minorities, many states of India establish laws that indirectly and disproportionately affect its Muslim citizens.

One such example include cow protection laws. Hinduism treats cows as sacred and holy; this, many states pass laws that are structured to protect cows. However, many Muslims rely on cattle farming, dairy farming, or meat industry-related roles for economic support. Thus, when laws meant to protect cattle are passed — such as banning the cattle trade, or limiting the sale of cattle — one group is disproportionately affected compared to the other.

One such example is the Maharashtra Animal Preservation Act of 2015, which prohibited cow slaughtering (Maharashtra Act No. IX of 1977).

Initially presented as legislation aimed at providing economic assistance to industries dependent on cow byproducts, this law carried severe penalties for non-compliance, with punishments ranging from 10 years to life imprisonment, varying based on the statutes of individual states. This ultimately results in greater prosecution and harm towards Muslim cattle ranchers (Patel 260). Furthermore, these laws also set a precedent to the idea of oppression and discrimination in the face of divergent beliefs, giving rise to “cow lynching”, where mobs violently target minorities in the name of cow protection. Similarly, police forces are accused of sitting by idly and allowing this to happen – “police often stalled prosecutions of the attackers, while several BJP politicians publicly justified the attacks” (Human Rights Watch).

7. Surveillance, Emergency Regulations, and Terrorism-Related Restrictions in India

Introduction

India's strategic position and diverse population have made it a focal point for complex security challenges, including terrorism. In response, the Indian government has implemented a range of surveillance measures, emergency regulations, and restrictions aimed at combating terrorism and maintaining national security. While these measures are crucial for safeguarding the populace, they have also raised concerns regarding civil liberties and the potential for abuse.

Surveillance Measures

  • Expansion of Surveillance: In recent years, India has significantly expanded its surveillance capabilities. This includes the installation of CCTV cameras in public areas, monitoring of phone calls and digital communications, and the use of advanced technology such as facial recognition systems. The Central Monitoring System (CMS), for instance, allows the government to access and intercept any digital communication in real-time, purportedly to monitor threats to national security (”Indian police use facial recognition to persecute Muslims and other marginalized communities”).
  • Use of Drones and Artificial Intelligence: The government has also started employing drones for surveillance in sensitive areas, particularly in regions prone to insurgency and border disputes. Furthermore, there is an increasing reliance on artificial intelligence to analyze data collected from various surveillance sources to predict and prevent potential terrorist activities.

Emergency Regulations

  • Armed Forces Special Powers Act (AFSPA): One of the most controversial pieces of legislation, AFSPA, grants special powers to the armed forces in regions that are considered "disturbed" due to insurgent activities. These powers include the authority to prohibit a gathering of five or more persons in an area, use force, or even open fire after giving due warning if they feel a person is acting against the law.
  • National Security Act (NSA): The NSA is another critical regulation allowing preventive detention of individuals if they are suspected of acting against national security. The act's broad scope, which allows detention without a charge for up to 12 months, has been widely criticized for its potential misuse against political dissidents and minority communities (India Govnmt.).

Terrorism-Related Restrictions

Restrictions on Movement and Communication: In high-security zones, particularly in conflict-affected areas like Jammu and Kashmir, the government frequently imposes restrictions on movement and communication. These include curfews, internet blackouts, and restrictions on mobile telecommunications, often justified by the need to prevent the organization of terror activities or the spread of harmful misinformation.

Legal Frameworks: India's legal framework to counter terrorism includes several laws, such as the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), which allows the state to deal with activities directed against the integrity and sovereignty of India. Amendments to UAPA have given the government sweeping powers to ban organizations and individuals as terrorists, which while strengthening security, also open avenues for rights abuses (India Govnmt.).

Conclusion

While the Indian government's surveillance and emergency regulations are designed to address genuine security concerns, they must be balanced with the need to protect civil liberties. The implementation of these measures has often led to debates over privacy violations and the suppression of dissent (Oxford Human Rights Hub). Moving forward, it is crucial for such measures to be accompanied by robust legal oversight and transparency to prevent abuse and ensure they do not undermine the democratic values that form the core of the Indian constitution.

8. Organizations and groups combating Islamophobia in India

Introduction

The increased feeling of Islamophobia in India has more than one body, organization, or group in proactive countering to the discrimination and propagation of inclusivity on religious lines. These entities are involved in legal advocacy, educational programs, and grassroots mobilization to meet the systemic and societal problems that Muslims of India face.

Notable Organizations and Their Approaches

Citizens for Justice and Peace (CJP):

Who They Are: CJP was founded by Teesta Setalvad, and it is a rights-based initiative focusing on due process and legislative protections through legal aid and advocacy to minority communities, particularly Muslims.

How They Counter Islamophobia: CJP works through legal advocacy to safeguard constitutional rights, provides legal aid to victims of hate crime and communal violence, and takes up initiatives to inform people about issues related to secularism and justice.

Methods Used: Legal representation, public campaigns, and workshops geared towards instilling an understanding of the necessity for interfaith harmony in the pursuit of harmonious living.

United Against Hate (UAH):

Who They Are: The initiative by a group of activists to fight back with the overwhelming number of hate crimes in India.

How They Fight against Islamophobia: UAH organizes public meetings, protests, offers legal help to the victimized by hate crimes, and brings issues like communal bias and violence to the forefront of public debates.

Methods Used: Mobilizing civil society through sensitization campaigns and the creation of networks of legal and psychological assistance to the victims.

Quill Foundation:

Who They Are: A research-based organization geared toward meeting the development needs of the marginalized communities with main focus on issues affecting the Muslim community.

How They Counter Islamophobia: Specializing in Islamic law, this center conducts extensive research on legal and social discrimination against Muslims, publishes reports, and lobbies for changes in policy.

Methodologies: Publishing peer-reviewed articles, policy advocacy, and academic partnerships to influence public opinion and policy.

Jamia Teachers' Solidarity Association (JTSA):

Who They Are: The group comprises faculty members of Jamia Millia Islamia University who mobilize around issues of justice and communal harmony.

How They Counter Islamophobia: JTSA organizes seminars and conferences for the very idea of education and dialogue on communal issues, research human rights abuses, and brings publication into being for cognizance.

Methods: Academic advocacy, public discourse initiatives, and community engagement programs.

Aman Biradari:

What They Are: A people's campaign for secularism, peace, and justice, this campaign promotes communal harmony and legal reforms to ensure justice to the victims of communal violence.

How They Try to Counter Islamophobia: Through their projects like Nyayagrah—legal and social support for those affected by mass violence—and Dil Se, which works on communal harmony and rehabilitation.

Means Applied: Workshops on community healing, legal aid; lobbying to draw public attention to enacting and changing policies that safeguard minority rights.

Conclusion

The organizations that are a furtherance to counter the problem of Islamophobia in India are an important part of the endeavor to carry the constitutional values of the nation forward for fostering a culture of inclusive growth. They may do so through legal, educational, or grassroots strategies in aiding the victims and promoting social change. However, success at this broadly depends on broader societal acceptance and political will towards addressing the root causes of Islamophobia. Continued advocacy, education, and legal reform have all been at the forefront of mitigation to discrimination and furthering a more inclusive society.

9. Success Cases and positive examples of resistance to Islamophobia in India

Introduction

In times of growing Islamophobia, one has to look toward the subcontinent to find some remarkable victories in India. These have come through courtroom wins, changed policy, and also from the grassroots, which act not only as precedents but also as inspirations for movements and legal battle against discrimination. Below, we describe some of the most powerful cases.

Successful Legal Cases

Hashimpura Massacre Verdict:

Acquittal in the Akshardham Temple Attack Case:

In a landmark judgment, the Delhi High Court convicted 16 former policemen in connection with the 1987 Hashimpura massacre in which 42 Muslim men were shot dead during communal riots in Meerut, Uttar Pradesh. This judgment comes after decades of long struggle by the survivors and the victims' families; that day was hailed as a great moment of justice and an important precedent in holding state actors accountable for communal violence (The Economic Times).

In 2014, the Supreme Court of India acquitted all the six men who had been convicted in connection with the 2002 Akshardham Temple attack in Gujarat, terming it the entire lack of evidence and improper prosecution. This has underlined not only the problems of wrongful convictions in cases of Muslims, but rather underlined the fair trial rights that so many lack (The Hindu).

Impactful Policy Changes

Prevention and Prohibition of Communal Violence Bill:

Amendments to the Waqf Act:

Though not enacted, this draft was one of the important attempts of some parts of the political factions and civil society towards framing the framework for preventing communal violence and protecting minorities. If realized, its emphasis on the accountability of public servants and reparations for the victims would even represent an overall progressive shift in policy discourse, albeit its legislative journey has remained stalled.

The Government of India amended the Waqf Act in 2013, and the purposes included making management in the Waqf properties transparent and accountable, which certainly had far-reaching impacts on the economic resource base of the Muslim Community in India. The incision of these changes was not to encroach upon the Waqf properties and not allow the revenues to be used for the welfare of the Muslim community.

Grassroots Activism and Advocacy

Shaheen Bagh Protests:

This symbol became iconic—people sitting at a peaceful sit-in protest against the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) and the National Register of Citizens (NRC), known to be discriminatory against Muslims. Led mostly by Muslim women, the protest drew national and international attention to the potential of non-violent civil disobedience, proving again the power of grassroots mobilization against policies (The Hindu).

Role of Universities and Student Movements:

Protesters belong to student groups at a few universities that include Jawaharlal Nehru University (JNU) and Aligarh Muslim University (AMU) and have, of course, been in the forefront of organizing protest actions against policies that are perceived to be essentially designed against Muslims. These movements had proved successful in mobilizing awareness and have contributed to the coming up of new generations of activists who swear to fight against the menace of communalism.

Conclusion

Examples like this bring this fact out very clear, varied, and dynamic manifestations of resistance against Islamophobia in India. From the courtrooms to the streets, these successes point out the tenacity and ability of both communities and activists in the fight for justice and equality. And, while the road ahead is still full of further challenges, these act as beacons of hope and models from which to draw courage in future efforts to make their society more truly inclusive and equable for all.
